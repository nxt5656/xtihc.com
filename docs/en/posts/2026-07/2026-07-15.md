# When Charity Is Reduced to “We Did Something”

After the controversy over Han Hong’s “just going through the motions” remark, a Zhihu question asked which criticisms of her charity foundation were reasonable and which were not. One long answer by the user “鸡脚芝士” began somewhere more basic than accounting terminology: what is a charitable foundation actually for?

The answer contains a deliberately jarring line: “Is it for charity? Yes, it does charity, but that is the least important part.” This is not a fact certified by a court or an audit. It is the author pushing the question to its uncomfortable edge: does spending money automatically mean that charity has been completed? If fame, connections, positions, supplier contracts, and organizational control are produced alongside the spending, who are the real beneficiaries?

The author first distinguishes private foundations from public-fundraising foundations, then describes several possible gains. Reputation is one: a donation can become a powerful personal calling card. Tax treatment is another: under certain conditions, money placed in a foundation may receive tax benefits. There is also asset separation, since donated property belongs legally to the foundation rather than to the individual. Finally comes control: whoever controls the board may have substantial influence over investments and projects. The answer summarizes the anxiety in a simple idea: what matters is not always who owns the money, but who can direct it.

That claim needs a clear qualification. The answer’s descriptions of tax rates, required charitable spending, asset protection, related-party investment, and succession are the author’s generalizations and hypotheticals, not legal advice from this article. The precise treatment of a foundation depends on current law, registration documents, audit reports, and contracts. Treating one answer’s figures as universal rules, or treating every foundation as a private vault, would be another form of laziness.

What makes the answer worth reading is its focus on control. The author imagines a foundation named after himself: a board made up of friends, projects selected by his own judgment, travel and publicity paid from foundation funds, and every step documented well enough to look proper. No money has to be pushed directly into a personal bank account for reputation and connections to return to the founder. They can accumulate quietly, one respectable transaction at a time.

Public fundraising creates an even sharper discomfort. The money comes from strangers, while the projects may be chosen through a network of familiar relationships. In the answer’s fictional “鸡脚芝士 Charity Foundation,” aid could be directed toward a place where a relative holds office. Every step could still be described as research, evaluation, and implementation. To an ordinary donor, however, it would remain difficult to tell whether this was public interest or a relationship network wearing a more respectable coat. On paper, everyone is doing something. In practice, who decides what gets done may matter more than whether anything was done at all.

The answer then turns to ambulance donations. It separates the possible beneficiaries: the foundation gains prestige; suppliers sell products; hospitals and emergency centers receive fixed assets; local governments complete part of a public-service task; ordinary patients may not experience an improvement of the same scale. The answer gives specific figures about Han Hong’s foundation, its ambulance count, spending, and results. This article has not independently verified each figure, so they should not be treated as established facts here. Their value is as a prompt to ask better questions: did the project solve the most urgent problem, is the path of benefit transparent, and did donated money reach those who needed it most?

Ordinary people’s expectation of charity is simple: I give my own money to someone who genuinely needs help. Foundations can certainly run complex public projects, and not every yuan can be handed directly to an individual. But complexity should not become an excuse for opacity, and legality should not be the period at the end of every question. The more often an organization says it is “legal and compliant,” the more reasonable it is to ask: compliant in what way, who benefits, and who bears the cost?

The answer does not finally prove a conclusion about Han Hong as an individual. It leaves a broader suspicion instead: once charity is packaged as a personal brand, donors are no longer looking only at recipients. They are also looking at a machine built around prestige, resources, and power. A machine may genuinely do good work, but that good work does not erase its operating costs, and it cannot replace an open, traceable, challengeable set of accounts.
