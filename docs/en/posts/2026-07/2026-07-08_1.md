# China's "Execution Line": Why Do Ordinary People Save Money So Desperately?

In various financial news reports, we constantly hear high-minded calls to "stimulate consumption," "boost domestic demand," and "release residents' excess savings." Pundits sit in spacious broadcasting studios, utterly baffled: why, even with bank deposit interest rates slashed to near zero, do Chinese citizens still prefer to lock their money in the bank rather than spend it?

The answer is actually very simple, and even somewhat brutal.

In video games, there is a term called the **"Execution Line"** or **"Execution Threshold"** (斩杀线). It means that when your character's health drops below a certain percentage, a single basic enemy skill triggers an instant-kill sequence, executing you immediately, bypassing all armor and standard defenses.

**For the vast majority of ordinary Chinese citizens, their refusal to spend and their desperate saving habit is because their real-life "Execution Line" is set extremely low. If they encounter job loss, a critical illness, or an accident, there is no strong social safety net to catch them. A minor ripple can instantly trigger an "executed" survival crisis.**

### 1. The Survival Balance Sheet Behind the Savings Frenzy

Let us first examine a set of **official statistical data** from the National Bureau of Statistics, the National Healthcare Security Administration, and relevant financial institutions.

As of **the end of 2024**, household savings in China reached an astronomical **151.25 trillion RMB**, with per capita savings breaking the **100,000 RMB** mark for the first time (averaging around 107,300 RMB). On a global scale, China's household savings rate has long remained at an exceptionally high level of **43% to 44%**.

Does this mean Chinese people truly have "too much money to spend"?

The moment you contrast this with the actual income distribution of the population, this illusion instantly evaporates.
According to the **2024 per capita disposable income group data** published by the National Bureau of Statistics:
*   **Low-Income Group (bottom 20% of the population, approx. 280 million people)**: The annual per capita disposable income is only **9,542 RMB**, which translates to an **average monthly disposable income of just 795 RMB** (approx. $110 USD).
*   **Lower-Middle-Income Group (next 20% of the population, approx. 280 million people)**: The annual per capita disposable income is **21,608 RMB**, which translates to **about 1,800 RMB per month**.
*   This means that **40% of China's entire population (nearly 560 million people)** has an average monthly disposable income of only **1,000 to 1,300 RMB** (approx. $140–$180 USD). This aligns precisely with the famous statistical statement that "600 million people earn about 1,000 RMB a month."

On the other hand, the national average per capita disposable income stands at a much higher **41,314 RMB**. This massive chasm reveals a deeper, more brutal structural reality.

### 2. Inside vs. Outside the System: The Inflated "Averages" and the Temperature Difference

When analyzing these "disposable income averages" and "savings averages," we must exclude a massive, highly privileged enclave—**those working "within the system" (体制内, i.e., government civil servants, public institution staff, and employees of state-owned enterprises [SOEs])**.

In China, this group exists in a highly sheltered economic bubble:
1.  **Immunity of the "Iron Rice Bowl"**: They enjoy virtual lifetime employment. Being laid off is almost impossible, completely shielding them from the daily terror of job insecurity experienced by private-sector workers.
2.  **Inflated Economic Data**: In addition to highly stable base salaries and bonuses, they enjoy massive "hidden benefits" that are not factored into regular after-tax cash calculations—such as fully or even excessively funded housing provident funds (which can reach up to 24% combined), comprehensive supplementary medical insurance, corporate annuities, and highly subsidized state cafeterias.
3.  **Near-Total Protection**: In terms of healthcare, civil servants and SOE employees enjoy top-tier employee medical insurance or secondary public reimbursement schemes. Their actual out-of-pocket medical costs are often capped at 5% or less. The savings in their accounts is true "discretionary cash" that can be freely spent on luxury and lifestyle items.

However, **the high incomes and extreme security of this public-sector elite are callously "averaged" in national statistics with the 2.8 billion people living on 795 RMB a month, and the hundreds of millions of private-sector workers who face sudden lay-offs daily.**

The resulting ledger looks decent on paper, but it masks the economic winter currently being endured by ordinary citizens outside the system. For those in the private market, their actual disposable income and median savings are far lower than the official averages, while the "Execution Line" they face is far more exposed and brutal.

### 3. An Extremely Low "Execution Line": Ruined by a Single Illness

Why must ordinary people outside the system arm themselves with this protective savings shield?

Because in this society, unless you belong to that select elite sheltered in the "public-sector greenhouse," the baseline safety net is paper-thin, and the real-life "Execution Line" is unforgivably low. Healthcare is the sharpest, most relentless execution blade.

Let us look at the **official healthcare statistics (2023–2024)**:
*   **High Out-of-Pocket Burden**: In 2023, China's total health expenditure was calculated at **9.06 trillion RMB**. Within this massive spending, **personal health expenditure (out-of-pocket cash payments by patients) accounted for a staggering 27.3%**. This means more than a quarter of the total medical burden is borne directly on the shoulders of individuals and families.
*   **The Deficiencies of Urban/Rural Resident Medical Insurance**: Unlike the heavily subsidized employee medical insurance enjoyed by the public sector, ordinary citizens (farmers, gig workers, children) enrolled in "Resident Medical Insurance" receive hospitalization fund payment ratios within the **policy scope** stable at only **around 70%**. This means that even under the most ideal "policy-reimbursable directory," patients must pay 30% out-of-pocket. Once out-of-directory imported drugs and advanced diagnostic procedures are included, the actual out-of-pocket share often rises to 50% or more.
*   **The Weight of Hospitalization Costs**: 2024 official data shows that the **average cost per hospitalization for basic medical insurance participants is 8,443.63 RMB**.
    For an ordinary person in the low-income group earning a monthly disposable income of **795 RMB**, **the average cost of a single hospitalization is equivalent to 10.6 months of their entire disposable income.**

If it is just a common cold or a minor injury, a family might pull through by draining a few months of savings. But what if it is a "fatal illness" like cancer or critical cardiovascular disease, which easily costs tens or hundreds of thousands of RMB?

On the other side of the 70% policy-scope reimbursement is a black hole of out-of-pocket expenses. For a family earning a few thousand RMB a month, whose household savings have just crossed the 100,000 RMB threshold, **this is a direct "one-shot execution" from which there is no recovery.**

Without the "privilege safety net" enjoyed inside the system, an ordinary private-sector middle-class family is separated from absolute poverty by a single major illness. A low-income family is separated from death by a single expensive hospitalization.

### 4. Unemployment and the Silent Defense

In addition to healthcare, another factor driving down the "Execution Line" of ordinary people is the volatility of the employment market.

In the absence of a robust unemployment welfare system (where most ordinary people cannot meet the complex, bureaucratic criteria to receive unemployment benefits, and gig workers and migrant workers are entirely excluded from unemployment insurance), losing a job means the absolute cutoff of income.

When you wake up every morning facing mortgage payments, car loans, utility bills, school tuition, and the threat of your medical and social security contributions being suspended, the only instinctive defense for those outside the system is to **compress your desires to the absolute limit**:
* Skip dining out, eat cheap delivery;
* Avoid shopping malls, browse Pinduoduo;
* Refuse to buy new cars, hold onto old phones, and absolutely do not buy real estate.

This desperate saving and aggressive refusal to spend is, in essence, ordinary people trying to artificially raise their life's "Execution Line" using the most primitive means at their disposal. They know all too well that when a crisis hits, the balance in their bank account is the only thing that will not lie to them.

### 5. Conclusion

As long as the social safety net remains paper-thin, as long as the massive temperature difference inside and outside the system persists, as long as a single illness can easily bankrupt a household, and as long as unemployment equates to a survival dead-end, the Chinese public will never spend freely.

So-called "high savings" is not a cultural preference of the Chinese people; it is a **passive defense mechanism and a silent protest** by ordinary people outside the system against a fragile existence.

The 151.25 trillion RMB in household deposits is not a symbol of affluence. In every single cent of those deposits, what is written is not wealth, but the anxiety and dread of 1.4 billion real, concrete human beings standing before a dangerously low "Execution Line."
