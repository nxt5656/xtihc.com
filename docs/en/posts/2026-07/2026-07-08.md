# The Weight of "Guanxi" and the Barometer of a City

In China, *Guanxi* (personal connections or relationships) is an inescapable underlying code. Whether you are in academia, business, or sitting at the very peak of the political hierarchy, everything in this massive social network seems to eventually hinge on these two characters.

### 1. The Hidden Code Behind Power

Even for supreme figures who look down from the zenith of power, every single step of their ascent up the political ladder is silently shaped and guided by the invisible force of *Guanxi*.

Let us turn our gaze back a few decades to the protagonist of this tale—a "princeling" who now stands at the absolute pinnacle of state power. The starting point of his political career was made possible because his father, a founding revolutionary leader, leveraged a deep comradeship with a military heavyweight to place his newly graduated son directly into the General Office of the Central Military Commission as a confidential secretary. This ironclad military connection granted him the invaluable credential of "active-duty military service."

Yet, networks of connection do not always run smoothly; sometimes they collide with the friction of rules and political pushback. When he chose to take off his military uniform and head to a small northern county to gain grassroots experience, his path to promotion was temporarily blocked by the provincial chief, a principled leader who detested "well-connected" candidates.

During this bottleneck, family connections once again demonstrated their formidable capacity for self-correction. His father deployed his vast network to smoothly transfer him to a wealthy southern coastal province. Here, his father's close friends and former subordinates took over the torch of mentorship. Even when he failed to secure a majority vote in local elections, performed mediocrely in governance, or even ranked at the very bottom in crucial internal party votes, he was continuously promoted by successive provincial party secretaries during his seventeen years in that province.

Ultimately, when he was selected as the sole successor to the grand political game, it was not only a victory of dynastic bloodline but also the product of compromise among rival factions—his long-held low-profile, gentle, and "harmless" image made him the "lowest common denominator" that satisfied the interests of all competing power bases.

Even at the highest echelons of this society, the transfer of power and the circulation of wealth are fundamentally driven by the deep-seated networks of family, bloodline, old comrades, and factional *Guanxi*.

### 2. "Why Don't I Need Connections to Get Things Done?"

Faced with this absolute definition of *Guanxi*, many young people living in first-tier or strong second-tier Chinese cities instinctively push back:

"I think life in Chinese cities is actually great now. When I renew my ID card, open a small shop, enroll my child in school, or book a hospital appointment, I can do it all on a mobile app with a single click. It is clean, efficient, and I don't need to pull any strings at all!"

This feeling is genuine, but it is also incredibly naive.

**In Chinese cities, if you don't need to find connections for your daily affairs, it absolutely does not mean this society no longer runs on *Guanxi*. It simply means you have not yet encountered things that *require* it.**

You don't need connections to renew your ID because it is a low-value, standardized administrative service with zero room for rent-seeking. You don't need connections to open a small noodle shop because it causes no ripples in the local tax revenue or power hierarchy. You don't need connections to enroll your child in an ordinary public school because it is a baseline public resource.

But the moment you attempt to cross the boundaries of your social class and touch the deeper mechanisms of resource allocation:
* Your child wants to get into the single most prestigious, highly coveted experimental school in the province;
* Your company wants to acquire a prime piece of commercial land in the city center, or bid for a municipal infrastructure project worth hundreds of millions;
* A family member gets entangled in a serious judicial dispute, where sentencing and penalties hinge on flexible, discretionary judicial windows...

At that exact moment, the digitized, friction-free world vanishes. In its place, you will find the same cold, heavy, and impenetrable wall that can only be breached by the key of *Guanxi*.

### 3. The Scale of *Guanxi* and the Potential of a City

If "everything runs on connections" is an unavoidable reality in China, can we assume that all Chinese cities are fundamentally the same, merely different shadows of the same "connection-based society"?

The answer is a resounding no. On the contrary, **the level at which *Guanxi* begins to operate is the single most accurate barometer of a city's development potential.**

We can categorize the things requiring *Guanxi* into three distinct tiers:
*   **Low Tier (Small Matters):** School enrollment, basic healthcare, ordinary administrative permits, fire safety inspections, or opening a small retail business without being harassed by local officials.
*   **Medium Tier (Medium Matters):** Normal tax incentive applications, standard government technology grants, mid-sized public tenders, or coordinating cross-departmental approvals.
*   **High Tier (Large Matters):** Pioneering institutional innovations that push beyond existing policy frameworks, massive industrial fund injections, strategic land-use zoning changes, or coordinating massive multinational projects.

We can observe a city's potential through this barometer:

In stagnant, backward regions or lower-tier cities, **the tentacles of *Guanxi* reach down to the absolute lowest levels**. In these places, a resident needs to find a connection in the bureau of commerce just to open a milk tea shop; they need a connection with a department head just to see a doctor for a common cold; they even need to pull strings and send gifts just to get a child into an ordinary kindergarten. In such cities, rules completely surrender to personal networks, and rent-seeking is omnipresent. This not only drains social efficiency but completely suffocates entrepreneurial energy and the living space for external talent. Such cities possess zero long-term development potential.

In contrast, in highly vibrant, high-potential modern metropolises, **the starting threshold of *Guanxi* is pushed incredibly high**. In cities like Shanghai, Shenzhen, or Hangzhou, from opening a shop and visiting a doctor to running mid-sized business operations and standard public bidding, everything is handled efficiently within a highly legalized, digitized, and rule-based framework. Because the institutional moat is wide enough and the "floor" of rules is high enough, low-level clerks and grassroots officials have no power to demand bribes or block processes.

Yet, this does not mean *Guanxi* has disappeared in these cities. Instead, the starting point where connections begin to matter resides high up in the stratosphere. Only when major corporations seek to challenge existing policy boundaries to pioneer business innovations, or when massive strategic resources require municipal or national coordination, does the *Guanxi* and leverage of elites come into play.

**Therefore, to judge a city's development potential, you should never look at whether it is "completely free of connections" (which is unrealistic in the current social ecology). Instead, you must look at how *large* the things are that require connections.**

*   The **smaller** the matters that require connections, the weaker the city's rules, the more decayed its governance, and the **lower** its development potential.
*   The **larger** the matters that require connections, the firmer the city's baseline rules, the more transparent its grassroots operations, and the **higher** its development potential.

This is the weight of *Guanxi* in China, and the hidden barometer that measures the future of its cities.
