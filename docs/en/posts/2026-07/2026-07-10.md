# Punishment Unknown, Majesty Unmeasured: The Contemporary Blind Box of Law

An ancient maxim dictates, "When punishment cannot be known, the majesty of the ruler cannot be measured, and thus the people will fear their superiors." Upon first reading, one might dismiss this as the decaying dregs of thousand-year-old Legalist tyranny. Yet, after enduring the vicissitudes of time, these twelve characters have somehow emerged as the most vivid portrait of this very land today. High up in the courts, majestic legal codes pile up like mountains, their clauses seemingly carrying fixed prices in a fortress of strict rules. However, when these codes descend to the provinces and villages, they transform into an absurd drama, adapting wildly to different locations and specific individuals.

The common people know that the law exists, but they often have no idea where its blade will fall. A humble street vendor might be considered a hardworking citizen in one city, yet merely crossing a territorial river turns him into a madman guilty of "picking quarrels and provoking trouble." Similarly, when demanding hard-earned unpaid wages, the powerless who dare utter a word of complaint are swiftly dealt with as disruptors of public order. But if someone has the right connections to lean on, they miraculously become the pitied victims deserving of official care. The ruler of the law appears to be made of highly elastic rubber; how far it stretches depends entirely upon the mood and personal ties of the one holding it. If there is a familiar face leading the way or a written "note" paving the path, a disastrous loophole can be gently smoothed over with a casual "do not let this happen again." But for the isolated and helpless grassroots, even a mistake the size of a needle's eye can be magnified into an unforgivable, monstrous crime.

This darkly comical discrepancy is not the result of magistrates with failing eyesight; rather, it is the exquisite core of how political power operates. Precisely because the boundaries of punishment are so blurred and the ultimate right to interpret the law is kept perpetually suspended, the ordinary citizen feels a lingering terror in every ordinary day. You never know which spoken word will cross the red line, or which step will plunge into a minefield. Consequently, extreme caution becomes an instinct for survival, and self-censorship turns into a daily mandatory lesson. This is exactly that "unmeasured majesty." It establishes its dominance not with a gleaming executioner's blade, but with an endless supply of uncertainty, domesticating everyone into sheep who dare not cross an invisible boundary.

How lamentable it is that, having dreamt of a grand future until this day, the so-called fairness and justice have degraded into a blind box filled with unknown variables. When the law is no longer a sturdy shield protecting the good, but instead degenerates into a lump of dough kneaded at will by power, that lofty, overbearing majesty is actually already riddled with holes. As for the commoners intimidated by these unpredictable punishments, is it true fear in their hearts, or a soul that has long grown completely cold? I suspect that even those holding the elastic ruler would not dare to ask too closely.
